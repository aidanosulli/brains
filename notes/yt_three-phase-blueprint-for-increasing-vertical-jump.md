# Three-Phase Blueprint for Increasing Vertical Jump and Dunking

> **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JVQW9H7gc0o
> **Date:** 2026-06-01

## Core Principles

- Genetics influences jumping ability, but improvement is always within your control regardless of starting point
- Strength, power, and tendon adaptation must be developed together — skipping any phase leaves performance on the table
- Consistency over years, not months, is the true differentiator between athletes who plateau and those who break records
- **You only fail if you stop** — plateaus are normal and often precede significant PRs

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### Three-Phase System

**Phase 1 — Build the Engine (Strength)**
- Goal: produce maximum force
- Two sub-phases:
  - *Hypertrophy*: grow the muscle
  - *Intensity*: train the nervous system to recruit those muscles more effectively
- Key muscles: hips, hamstrings, calves, quads
- Target: 2–3 lifting sessions per week
- Shorter athletes (≤5'9") must be *stronger relative to bodyweight* than taller athletes to achieve equivalent vertical output

**Phase 2 — Build the Springs (Tendons & Nervous System)**
- Tendons store and release energy via the **stretch-shortening cycle** (fast eccentric → fast concentric)
- Forces during jumping can exceed **10× bodyweight** — requires strength base to avoid injury
- Progression protocol:
  - Start with **1 jump session/week**
  - Increase volume by ~**10% per week**
  - Build toward **2 sessions/week** for most adults; young athletes may handle 3–4
  - Run concurrently with Phase 1 lifting
- Most specific plyometric: **max-effort approach jumps** (e.g., attempting dunks on an adjusted rim)

**Phase 3 — Supercharge with Olympic Lifts (Force Velocity Bridge)**
- Problem it solves: high strength but slow force production (velocity deficit)
  - Ground contact time during jumping: ~0.2 seconds
  - Isometric peak force takes ~0.5 seconds — too slow if untrained for speed
- Solution: **Power cleans**
  - Ground contact time of the double knee bend closely mirrors jump plant contact time
  - Motor pattern of first pull → double knee bend mimics penultimate step → plant
  - Heavy catch overloads the eccentric phase
  - Correlated most strongly with vertical jump in a study of 1,000+ athletes (researcher: Satiar Ferogi)

### Force-Velocity Curve
- Heavier loads = slower movement speed; lighter loads = faster movement
- Training across the full curve (max strength → Olympic lifts → plyometrics → jumping) prevents plateau and bridges gym strength to athletic expression

### Vertical Jump Deficit Training
- Three common deficits athletes have:
  1. Weak (low max force)
  2. Slow (poor rate of force development)
  3. Poor jump technique
- Identify and address your *primary* deficit first before general programming

## Actionable Advice

- **Lower the rim** to a height where dunking is possible and practice max-effort jumps regularly — intent is the mechanism, not perfection
- Implement **variety in training** to prevent adaptation plateau; avoid staying in one modality (e.g., only strength or only plyometrics)
- **Learn the power clean** — ~20 minutes is sufficient for a beginner to grasp the basics; the motor learning transfers directly to jump mechanics
- Build jump volume gradually using the **10% weekly increase rule** — do not jump high-frequency from the start
- Go to oldest YouTube videos of elite athletes to see their starting point — useful for resetting expectations and building motivation
- Combine strength and jumping regularly; never fully separate them (avoid block periodization)

## Things to Avoid

- **Block periodization** — doing all strength work, then switching entirely to jumping, with nothing in between; worst periodization approach for vertical jump
- **Jumping high-frequency without a strength base** — leads to overuse injury (e.g., chronic knee pain from excessive early jumping without adequate strength)
- **Ignoring the force-velocity gap** — assuming a high squat alone will translate to a high vertical without training rate of force development
- **Quitting during plateaus** — multi-year plateaus are documented even at elite level; PRs often come *after* extended flat periods
- Using a taller, longer-legged athlete as your strength benchmark if you are shorter — you need *relatively more* strength

## Key Takeaways

- Vertical jump improvement requires three concurrent elements: **maximal strength, tendon/SSC development, and rate of force development** — training only one or two is insufficient
- The **power clean** is the single best gym exercise for vertical jump due to its timing, motor pattern overlap, and eccentric overload
- Progress is not always visible — tendons, nervous system wiring, and muscle size continue adapting even when tested performance stalls
- **Short athletes must prioritise relative strength** more aggressively than taller counterparts to achieve comparable jumping output
- Long-term commitment (often **5–10 years**) separates elite jumpers from those who plateau; squat PRs precede vertical PRs by ~5 years on average in elite populations
