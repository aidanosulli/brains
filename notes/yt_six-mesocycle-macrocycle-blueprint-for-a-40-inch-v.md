# Six-Mesocycle Macrocycle Blueprint for a 40-Inch Vertical Leap

> **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Icml891MLHA
> **Date:** 2026-07-06

CONFIDENCE: medium

## Core Principles

- **Fatigue-Fitness Model**: Performance is a function of fitness minus fatigue. High training loads build fitness but also accumulate fatigue; macrocycles exploit this by sequencing adaptations so fatigue dissipates and fitness peaks at the right time
- **Progressive Overload**: Each mesocycle builds on the previous one — volume → intensity → strength → power → speed → specificity
- **Potentiation via Accessory Work**: Accessory lifts in each cycle are deliberately chosen to prime the *next* cycle's main movements
- **Specificity-Generality Balance**: As specific work becomes more intense, general work must become proportionally easier to prevent overuse injury and neural burnout
- **Variety, Individuality, and Recovery**: No two athletes train identically; deload weeks and rest are non-negotiable components

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### The 6-Month Macrocycle (6 Mesocycles)

**Mesocycle 1 — Volume Building (Month 1)**
- Rep range: 8–12; Sets: 2–5; Intensity: 65–70%
- Deload built into week 4
- Focus on *general* work (does not closely mimic jumping)
  - Two-foot jumpers: sumo deadlift, unilateral work
  - One-foot jumpers: deep squats, full range of motion
- Frequency: 3 lifting days/week (e.g., Mon/Wed/Fri)
- Power exercises: ~4 reps × 5 sets
- Squats: 4–5 sets × 6–12 reps
- Accessory: 8–14 reps depending on athlete fitness
- General days (Tue/Thu): varied movement circuits to build broad athletic capacity

**Mesocycle 2 — Intensification (Month 2)**
- Shift toward more specific work
- Clean pulls: ~6 sets × 3–5 reps at ~90%
- Power cleans: 75–85%, reps drop from 4 to ~3, sets increase from 5 to 7
- Main lifts move to 6–8 rep range
- Accessory begins to prime Mesocycle 3 (e.g., RDLs and calf raises at 4–6 reps, ~85% intensity)
- General days: bodybuilding circuits (lower and upper body) + tempo running (improves body composition and cardiorespiratory fitness)

**Mesocycle 3 — Absolute/Max Strength (Month 3)**
- Back squat: 10×3 or 10×2
- Clean pulls at 105–110% of power clean max
- Accessory introduces *eccentric strength* as a primer for Mesocycle 4:
  - Nordic hamstring curls
  - Two-up/one-down calf raises
- General days: jump circuits in sand, 20–30 seconds, varied jumps (tuck jumps, toe-touch jumps, single-leg hops); tempo runs 30–45 sec with 3-min rest — keep intensity sub-maximal

**Mesocycle 4 — Strength-Speed / Accentuated Eccentrics (Month 4)**
- Power clean pushed to absolute maxes; power clean 2×/week, clean pull 1×/week
- Accentuated eccentrics introduced:
  - Belt squat: assisted up, controlled down at 110–115% of back squat max
  - Eccentric split squats: 3 build-up sets, then 3 working sets of 2–3 reps
- Partial ranges of motion introduced (specific to jump type)
- Accessory shifts to elastic/high-velocity qualities:
  - High-velocity calf raises, drop RDLs, bouncing calf raises
  - ~5–6 reps × 2 sets (avoid exceeding this to limit neural fatigue)
- General days become more general again: bodyweight circuits, reduced tempo work, jump circuits removed

**Mesocycle 5 — Speed-Strength / Bar Speed (Month 5)**
- Olympic lift focus: power snatch (~50% of power clean max), lighter power cleans (~60%), hang power cleans introduced
- Squat approach: primer sets (5-4-3 up to ~85%), then high-velocity squats at 65–70% (timed for speed)
- Accessory primes Mesocycle 6: med ball throws, shot puts, low plyo volumes (step-off box, step-up jumps)
- General days become maximally general — static flexibility, bodyweight core, barefoot 100m walk-back runs; all jump circuits removed

**Mesocycle 6 — Peak Specificity (Month 6)**
- Highest specificity and plyo volume (intensity and variety, not raw volume)
- Option A (Load Management): Jump Mon/Thu; "feel-good" lifts Tue/Fri (slow squats, slow calf raises, RDLs — very easy)
- Option B: Shock loading Mon/Wed/Fri with sprints and plyo emphasis
- Accessory becomes maximally general: slow concentric RDLs, slow calf raises — keeps nervous system fresh and maintains tendon/joint health

### Post-Macrocycle Taper
- Reduce total volume by ~50%
- Increase intensity: max effort jumping more frequently
- Squats: 5-4-3-2-1 protocol
- Power clean: daily velocity maxing
- Maintain but don't push general work volumes

### Jump Session Progression (40-Minute Total Window)
| Mesocycle | Low-Effort Warm-Up | Max-Effort Jumping |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 35 min | 5 min |
| 2 | 30 min | 10 min |
| 3 | 25 min | 15 min |
| 4+ | 20 min | 20 min (cap here) |

- Exceeding the 20-min max-effort cap causes performance flatness in subsequent sessions

## Actionable Advice

- Train 3 days/week for lifting, use remaining days for general/recovery work
- Always start mesocycles on the lower end of set ranges and build across weeks
- Use clean pulls at 105–110% of power clean to build first- and second-pull strength, making the power clean feel easier in the following cycle
- Introduce tempo running on general days early in the macrocycle; reduce and eventually eliminate it as specificity increases
- Use a stopwatch to enforce bar speed on high-velocity squat sets (5 reps at 65–70%, as fast as possible)
- Completely remove jump circuits from general days by Mesocycle 5 to protect joints and CNS
- Keep elastic/velocity accessory work to ≤2 sets of 5–6 reps to control neural fatigue

---

## Things to Avoid

- **Jumping higher every week**: Performance fluctuates due to the fatigue-fitness model — this is expected, not a sign of failure
- **Too much specificity too soon**: Movements that closely mimic jumping too early in the macrocycle increase injury risk
- **Exceeding elastic/velocity accessory volume**: More than 2 sets of 5–6 reps leads to excessive neural fatigue
- **Keeping general days intense while specific days are also intense**: Must balance specificity up with generality up on off days — running both simultaneously causes injury and burnout
- **Excessive max-effort jumping**: Beyond the 20-min cap per session causes training flatness
- **Neglecting the deload**: Week 4 of Mesocycle 1 (at minimum) must include a deload

## Key Takeaways

- A 6-month macrocycle with 6 sequential mesocycles — volume → intensification → max strength → strength-speed → speed-strength → peak specificity — is the structural backbone of elite vertical jump development
- Each cycle's accessory work deliberately primes the *next* cycle's main lifts, creating stacked adaptations rather than isolated gains
- The specificity-generality balance is a dynamic lever: as specific work intensifies, general work must become correspondingly easier to prevent overtraining and injury
- Accentuated eccentric loading (110–115% of max) and high-velocity work are key training tools that directly target the primary determinants of jumping (eccentric strength and stretch-shortening cycle)
