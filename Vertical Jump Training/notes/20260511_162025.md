# Vertical Jump Training: 25-Minute Protocol for Adding Inches Fast

## Core Principles

- **"Do the thing"** — the primary training stimulus should be the exact movement you're trying to improve (vertical jumping)
- You cannot effectively train toward a goal without first knowing your baseline — measurement is non-negotiable
- Progress is tracked by a simple loop: **measure → work → remeasure**
- This principle applies beyond athletics — to any skill or life goal
- Both takeoff legs should be trained equally, regardless of dominant jumping style

---

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### The Three-Part Protocol (~25–30 minutes total)

**Part 1: Do the Thing (5–10 min)**
- Stand directly under the net/hoop
- Jump as high as possible on every rep, attempting to beat the previous jump
- This is *not* endurance work — rest 90–120 seconds when fatigued, then continue
- Goal: maximum effort each jump

**Part 2: Two-Step Approach Isolation (10 min)**
- Start in an athletic/squatted posture
- Fall into the jump and take exactly two steps → explode up
- Focus: managing the horizontal-to-vertical energy transfer at ground contact
- 5 minutes leading with right foot forward, 5 minutes with left foot forward

**Part 3: Full Approach Jumps (10 min)**
- Tie together the dialled-in two-step with a full run-up
- 5 minutes approaching from each side (left-right and right-left)
- Works both one-foot and two-foot jumpers

### Measurement Options (Low-Tech)

1. **Tape method:** Mark standing reach on wall with tape → jump and slap a second piece of tape → measure the gap
2. **Backboard reference:** Film yourself jumping and touching the backboard — use it as a visual reference point even without exact numbers
3. **Jump mat** (gym equipment): Most accurate; not required

### Training Schedule
- Minimum: 3 days/week
- Optimal: up to 6 days/week (Pat's protocol that produced +5.3 inches in 30 days)
- Remeasure every **2 weeks**

---

## Actionable Advice

- Record your standing reach and jump reach *before* starting any training
- If no tape measure is available, use a basketball hoop as a visual reference point and film it
- Begin *every* workout with 5–10 minutes of pure vertical jumping before any other work
- Train both legs in the two-step and approach phases — even committed one-foot jumpers benefit from bilateral work
- Remeasure at the 2-week mark to objectively assess progress and adjust if needed

---

## Things to Avoid

- Starting training without an established baseline measurement
- Treating the jumping sets as endurance/cardio — jumping when fatigued produces low-quality reps; **rest between sets**
- Skipping the two-step isolation — this is where the horizontal-to-vertical transfer happens and is commonly neglected
- Only training the dominant takeoff leg

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Measurement drives progress** — establish a baseline before training; remeasure every 2 weeks to validate what's working
- **Specificity is king** — the bulk of vertical jump training should be *vertical jumping*, not just accessory strength work
- **The two-step is the critical link** — isolating the final two steps trains the most important mechanical phase of the jump
- **Simplicity works** — a 25–30 minute protocol of three progressively backed-up jump variations, done 3–6x/week, produced +5.3 inches in 30 days for a 5'8" athlete
- **Both sides matter** — train approach jumps from both sides to eliminate asymmetry and maximise overall jump height
