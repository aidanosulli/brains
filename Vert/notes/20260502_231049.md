# Blueprint to a 40-Inch Vertical Leap — John Evans / THP Strength

## Core Principles

- **Progressive overload is non-negotiable** — programs must continuously increase stimulus over time
- **Fatigue-Fitness Model** — high training load builds fitness but also fatigue; performance peaks only when fatigue dissipates. Weekly performance fluctuations are normal and expected
- **Stacking adaptations** — each mesocycle is designed to potentiate the next; no cycle exists in isolation
- **Specificity vs. Variety balance** — general work builds the foundation; specific work peaks performance. Too much specificity too soon causes injury
- **The General-Specific Seesaw** — as specific days get more intense/specific, general days must become *more* general to compensate and prevent overtraining
- **Variety, individuality, and recovery** are core pillars alongside progressive overload

---

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### Macrocycle Structure
- **Duration:** 6 months (one macrocycle = 6 mesocycles)
- Each mesocycle ≈ 1 month, with a **deload in week 4** of each cycle
- Training frequency: **3 lifting days/week** (e.g. Mon/Wed/Fri) + **2 general days** (Tue/Thu)

---

### Mesocycle Breakdown

#### Month 1 — Volume Building (General Preparatory)
- **Goal:** Build base volume with general movements
- **Reps:** 8–12 | **Sets:** 2–5 | **Intensity:** 65–70%
- Power exercise: ~4 reps × 5 sets
- Squat: 4–5 sets × 6–12 reps
- Accessory: 8–12 reps (up to 14 for fit athletes)
- **Movement type:**
  - Two-foot jumpers → sumo deadlift, unilateral work
  - One-foot jumpers → deep squats, full ROM movements
- General days: variety of movements, bodybuilding circuits

#### Month 2 — Intensification (Transition to Specific Work)
- **Goal:** Increase intensity; begin sport-specific movements
- Clean pulls / power clean introduced
  - Clean pulls: 6 sets × 3–5 reps @ ~90%
  - Power clean: 75–85%
- Squat: move toward 6–8 rep range
- Accessory: shift toward specific movements (RDLs, calf raises @ 4–6 reps, up to 85% intensity)
- General days: bodybuilding circuits + **tempo running** (30–45 sec runs, ~3 min rest; sub-lactic effort)

#### Month 3 — Absolute Strength
- **Goal:** Peak strength base before transitioning to power
- Squat: **10 sets × 2–3 reps** (back squat)
- Clean pulls: **105–110% of power clean max** (builds first/second pull strength)
- Accessory: **Eccentric strength primer** — Nordic hamstring curls, 2-up/1-down calf raises
- General days: **jump circuits in sand** (20–30 sec, varied jumps — tuck jumps, toe touch, single-leg spin hops)
- Tempo work increases: more sets/reps

#### Month 4 — Eccentric Strength & Bar Speed (Power Development)
- **Goal:** Move heavy loads fast; develop eccentric strength for jump mechanics
- Power clean: max effort twice/week; clean pull on third day
- **Accentuated eccentrics:**
  - Belt squat with partner assistance: loads at **110–115% of back squat max**
  - Eccentric split squats (safety bar, supramaximal): 3 build-up sets + 3 working sets × 2–3 reps
- Partial ROM and specificity increases (one-foot → block pulls; two-foot → full ROM floor pulls)
- Accessory: **elastic/high-velocity work** — bouncing calf raises, drop RDLs (~5–6 reps × 2 sets max)
- General days: return to bodybuilding circuits; **remove tempo and jump circuits** to protect tendons/joints

#### Month 5 — Bar Speed / Speed-Strength
- **Goal:** Convert strength to speed; move bars as fast as possible
- Power clean: ~60% for velocity
- Power snatch: ~50% of power clean max
- Hang power cleans introduced (loads stretch-shortening cycle on OLIs)
- Squat: blend max strength + high-velocity — primer sets (5-4-3 up to 85%), then timed velocity sets (~65–70%, 5 reps as fast as possible)
- Accessory: **shock loading primer** — med ball throws (overhead, back, between legs, forward), low plyo volumes (box step-offs, step-up jumps)
- General days: **fully general** — static stretching, bodyweight circuits, core work, barefoot 100m walks/runs; no jumping whatsoever

#### Month 6 — Peak Specificity
- **Goal:** Maximum specificity; peaking for performance
- High plyo volume and intensity (highest point of entire macrocycle)
- Sprints incorporated heavily
- Load management option: jump Mon/Thu → "feel good" lifts Tue/Fri (slow squats, slow calf raises, RDLs — very easy)
- Accessory: becomes **maximally general** — slow concentric RDLs, slow calf raises; keeps nervous system fresh
- After completion: **taper** — cut volume ~50%, increase intensity; max effort jumping more frequently; 5-4-3-2-1 squat protocols; daily velocity maxing on power clean

### Jump Session Progression (40-Minute Total Window)
Progress by shifting 5 minutes from warm-up to max effort each mesocycle:

| Mesocycle | Warm-Up (Low Effort) | Max Effort |
|-----------|----------------------|------------|
| Month 1 | 35 min | 5 min |
| Month 2 | 30 min | 10 min |
| Month 3 | 25 min | 15 min |
| Month 4+ | 20 min | 20 min ← **cap here** |

- Do **not** exceed 20 min of max effort jumping — leads to flatness and poor session quality

## Actionable Advice

- **Start general, finish specific** — use general preparatory work early to build a foundation you'll intensify later
- **Build in a deload every 4th week** of each mesocycle without exception
- **Use clean pulls at 105–110% of power clean max** during the strength phase to prime the second pull and make power cleans feel easier
- **Cap elastic/high-velocity accessory work at ~2 sets × 5–6 reps** — neural fatigue accumulates quickly beyond this
- **Watch tempo runs carefully** — keep them sub-lactic (no burning legs); purpose is fitness, not fatigue
- **Reference Boo Schexnayder's work** on plyo variety (podcast worth watching)
- When peaking (Month 6), use a load management split rather than trying to jump and lift heavy on the same days

---

## Things to Avoid

- **Being too specific too early** — movements that closely mimic jumping too soon in the cycle lead to injury (back, knee, Achilles)
- **Increasing max effort jumping beyond 20 minutes** per session — causes flatness and diminishing returns
- **High neural load on both specific and general days simultaneously** — if specific days are intense, general days *must* be easy
- **Trying to PR every week** — the fatigue-fitness model means performance naturally fluctuates; chasing weekly PRs breaks the system
- **Ignoring the primer function of accessory work** — accessory lifts in each cycle should prepare the body for what the *next* cycle demands
- **Skipping variety** — monotonous programming caps adaptation; variety is a core pillar

---

## Key Takeaways

- A **6-month macrocycle with 6 sequential mesocycles** is the optimal structure for maximising vertical jump — each phase builds on and potentiates the next
- **Specificity must be earned** — general work in early phases creates the foundation that specific, high-velocity, and plyometric work in later phases can express
- **Eccentric strength is a major determinant of jumping ability** — accentuated eccentrics (110–115% of max) should be a deliberate training tool
