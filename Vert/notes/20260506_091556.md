# Bosco Test for Plyometric Prescription Customisation

## Core Principles

- Individual response to plyometric training varies significantly — a one-size-fits-all prescription is suboptimal
- The Bosco Test identifies an athlete's **dominant muscle function profile**, allowing plyometric volume, intensity, and type to be matched to their physiology
- Plyometric effectiveness depends on how well an athlete utilises the **stretch-shortening cycle (SSC)**

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### The Bosco Test Protocol
- Involves a series of jump tests to assess SSC function and fatigue resistance:
  - **Squat Jump (SJ)** — measures pure concentric power (no SSC)
  - **Countermovement Jump (CMJ)** — measures fast SSC utilisation
  - **Repeated Jumps / 15–60 sec continuous jumping** — measures SSC endurance and fatigue resistance

### Athlete Profiles Derived from Testing
- **SJ > CMJ** → Poor SSC utilisation; needs reactive/plyometric training emphasis
- **CMJ > SJ** → Good SSC utilisation; can handle more advanced plyometric loading
- **High fatigue index on repeated jumps** → Lower tolerance for high-volume plyometric work; needs progressive build-up
- **Low fatigue index** → High SSC endurance; can sustain greater plyometric volumes

### Prescription Logic
- Match **plyometric type** (e.g., slow vs. fast SSC exercises) to athlete profile
- Match **volume and density** to fatigue tolerance revealed by the repeated jump test
- Re-test periodically to track adaptation and update prescription

## Actionable Advice

- Administer all three test components (SJ, CMJ, repeated jumps) before designing a plyometric programme
- Use the **CMJ–SJ difference (%)** as a direct indicator of SSC efficiency — higher % = better SSC use
- For athletes with poor SSC utilisation, prioritise **reactive drills and short ground contact exercises** (e.g., pogo jumps, ankle stiffness drills) before progressing to depth jumps
- For athletes with high fatigue indices, **reduce plyometric density** (more rest between sets/sessions) and build volume conservatively
- Reassess every mesocycle (4–6 weeks) to determine readiness to progress

## Things to Avoid

- Prescribing identical plyometric programmes to all athletes regardless of SSC profile
- Overloading athletes with high fatigue indexes with excessive plyometric volume — increases injury risk without proportional benefit
- Progressing to **fast SSC / high-intensity plyometrics** (depth jumps, bounding) before an athlete demonstrates adequate SSC efficiency
- Ignoring the SJ–CMJ comparison — missing this misses the foundational diagnostic insight of the test

## Key Takeaways

- The Bosco Test provides an objective, individualised baseline for plyometric programming rather than relying on guesswork
- The CMJ vs. SJ comparison reveals SSC efficiency — the single most important variable for choosing plyometric exercise type
- Repeated jump testing exposes fatigue tolerance, directly informing safe volume thresholds
- Athletes with poor SSC use need **more** plyometric training emphasis, but with *lower intensity* exercises first
- Periodic re-testing ensures programming evolves with the athlete's actual adaptation, not assumed progress
