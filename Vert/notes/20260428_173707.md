# Vertical Leap Blueprint: Breaking the 30-Inch Barrier

**Source:** John Evans (coach to 4 of 6 individuals tested over 50" vertical, 2,000+ athletes coached)

---

## Core Principles

- **Impulse = Force × Time**: To generate a change in momentum, you need both force (speed) and time (depth/lowness in the approach)
- **Eccentric forces drive concentric output**: High approach speed creates high eccentric loading, which powers a greater concentric (upward) force
- **Time-specificity of strength matters**: Jumping occurs in 170–300ms; a heavy squat can take >1,000ms — raw strength alone doesn't transfer unless it's expressed at the right speed
- **Nervous system freshness is a performance limiter**: Jumping demands massive neural output (eccentric forces in one-foot jumping can exceed 13× body weight); overtraining degrades jump quality
- **30 inches is not a genetic ceiling** — it reflects training limitations, not physical potential

---

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### The Three Root Causes of Being Stuck Under 30"
1. **Insufficient technique** — not low enough and/or not fast enough in the approach
2. **Lack of rate of force development (RFD)** — strength exists but not at the time intervals relevant to jumping
3. **Sessions are too long** — nervous system fatigue prevents high-quality force production

### Six-Week Training Cycle Structure
- **Weeks 1–2:** Absolute strength (deep barbell back squat, hinge, heavy calf raises)
  - Baseline target: squat ≥1.6× body weight
- **Weeks 3–4:** Power work — Olympic lifting derivatives (clean pulls, power cleans), hex bar jumps
  - Intensity: 70–85% 1RM, every rep with 100% violent intent
- **Weeks 5–6:** Plyometrics — depth jumps, alternate leg bounds
  - Low rep ranges, maximum quality only

> This sequence leverages residual training effects from each block, building potentiation cumulatively.

### Two-Foot Jump Technique
- Shorten the approach initially; **accelerate late, not early**
- 2–3 steps out: lean shoulders forward, hips behind
- **Penultimate step** (second to last): plant close to under hips, flat-footed, in a semi-squat (think: low lunge from the side)
- Drive the knee of the penultimate leg forward; maintain ~90° bend in the front leg — not a straight-leg bound
- At toe-off: big split between legs visible from the side; body traveling **downward and forward**, head does not pop up
- Reposition block foot to be **knee-to-knee** just before it plants
- Drive block foot aggressively into the floor and jump **straight up**, not forward

### One-Foot Jump Technique
- **Rhythm is king**: start slow, accelerate in the last 2 strides
- Aim for ~5-step approach; final stride should be **longer** than typical
- Penultimate step knee should be **behind the hips** (viewed from the side)
- Dip the knee toward the ground through the penultimate step (low lunge shape)
- On takeoff: throw foot out in front, **lean back slightly** — shoulders behind hips to convert horizontal force to vertical
- Do **not** drive the takeoff knee forward (unlike two-foot jumping)

### Warm-Up Protocol
1. **Locomotion drills**: A skips, B skips, fast legs, cycling drill, low-level bounding
2. **Dynamic flexibility**: toe touches, deep bodyweight squats, long lunges, arm circles, side squats
3. **Progressive jump intensity ramp**: 10% → 20% → 30% → … → 100% over 5–15 minutes
4. **Max effort jumps**: only once at 80–100% — apply all technical cues

## Actionable Advice

- **Two-foot drill — cone drill**: Place two cones ~6 feet apart; plant penultimate step at cone 1, push off aggressively with full hip/knee extension, plant block foot at cone 2 and jump straight up. Progress distance to 7, 8, 9, 10 ft as technique improves. Add a box at cone 2 to reinforce vertical intent
- **One-foot drill — step-back build**: Stand one stride from target (rim/vert trainer), takeoff leg back, roll through penultimate step, drive knee and arms hard upward. Add one step at a time only when the previous distance feels controlled. Regress if adding a step reduces height. Aim to work back to a full 5-step approach
- **Olympic lifting for RFD**: Learn clean pulls and power cleans; once technique is solid, train at 70–85% with maximal intent. The double knee bend (150–200ms) closely mimics jump ground contact time intervals
- **Cut sessions at ~30 minutes** or when vertical drops 5–10% from peak — whichever comes first
- **Monitor tendon pain**: Never let pain exceed 2.5/10 during a session. If entering a session at 2–2.5/10, substitute **depth drops** (step off a 24–36" box, 10–15 reps) instead of max-effort jumping

## Things to Avoid

- **Running fast too early in the approach** — causes poor rhythm and incorrect position at takeoff
- **Straight-leg penultimate step** — keep knee at ~90°, not a stiff-leg bound
- **Head popping upward** during the penultimate-to-block transition — body should travel downward and forward
- **Jumping forward instead of straight up** in two-foot drills
- **Maxing out squat strength without addressing RFD** — Isaiah Rivera case: power cleaned 330 lbs, squatted 440 lbs, yet ground contact time *increased* and vertical *dropped* because the 70–85% speed-strength zone was neglected
- **Starting plyometrics too early in a cycle** — plyos should come last to take advantage of potentiation from strength and power blocks
- **High-volume plyometrics** — excessive volume causes the same nervous system downregulation as over-jumping
- **Ignoring tendon pain signals** — residual pain degrades training quality across multiple subsequent sessions
- **Sessions without a plan or cutoff** — jumping for hours is counterproductive; unlike sprinting, jumping offers no natural ground-contact constraint to self-regulate volume

---

## Key Takeaways

- **The three limiters under 30"** are technique (not low/fast enough), insufficient rate of force development, and overly long sessions — not genetics
- **Speed in the approach is non-negotiable**: high approach velocity generates the eccentric forces that power an elite jump; slow approach = low ceiling regardless of gym strength
- **Strength must be expressed at the right speed**: build a base (squat ≥1.6× BW), then shift to 70–85% Olympic lifting and power work with violent intent — this bridges gym strength to actual jump performance
- **Nervous system management is the hidden variable**: fresh, short, high-quality sessions outperform long grind sessions every time
- **Technique fixes produce rapid, near-guaranteed gains**: correcting approach rhythm, penultimate step depth, and block foot mechanics alone is enough to deliver 2–4" improvement within 6 weeks
