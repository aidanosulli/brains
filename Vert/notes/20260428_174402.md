# ATG Jump Training: Complete Guide to Dunking & Vertical

## Core Principles

- **Three-stage framework**: Sled Balance → Squat Balance → Jump Balance — mastering all three covers everything meaningful about jumping higher
- **Six zones of lower body training**: The ankle, knee, and hip each have two directions (flex and extend) — 6 zones total. Most athletes only train 2 of 6
- **Pain-free foundation first**: Getting the knees pain-free in jumping positions alone helps many people jump higher
- **Strength through full range of motion** is more valuable than heavy partial-range training for jumping
- **Upper body matters**: Arm swing is a significant differentiator between running/jumping athletes — free arms = more force into the ground
- **Jump balance is underdeveloped**: Most athletes are significantly weaker off one leg than the other (up to 13 inches difference measured), limiting both performance and injury resilience

---

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### Stage 1 — Sled Balance (Pain-Free Foundation)
- **Forward sled**: Long steps, think about jumping every step; strengthens feet, knees over toes, glutes
- **Backward sled**: Quick steps; builds knee-over-toe strength in the position where knees hurt during jumping; responsible for getting off painkillers
- **No sled alternatives** (in order of accessibility):
  - Backward treadmill (unpowered, variable resistance)
  - Knee-over-toe calf raise (bodyweight, control the descent)
  - Backward step-up (progress by increasing step height)

### Stage 2 — Squat Balance (Full-Range Strength)
Six zones applied to the squat pattern:
1. **Knee extension** — full-depth squat; add chains to load the bottom AND top of the movement simultaneously (cheat code: chains allow heavy load through full ROM)
2. **Knee flexion** — Nordic curl / natural leg curl progression (bench-assisted → full)
3. **Hip extension** — full-range seated good morning
4. **Hip flexion** — weighted hip flexor raises (huge differentiator between weight room athletes and natural jumpers)
5. **Ankle plantarflexion** — heavy single-leg calf raise through full ROM (44 lb example used); elite jumpers have outlier ankle strength
6. **Ankle dorsiflexion** — addressed via sled/step-up work

- **Split squat** (front foot elevated = easier): Master with high reps, bodyweight first — even elite jumpers can gain from this if it's a weak point

### Stage 3 — Jump Balance (All Four Plants)
Four jump plant combinations to master:
- **Right-Left plant** (dominant for most)
- **Left-Right plant**
- **Right leg only**
- **Left leg only**

Progression:
- Start with rim taps off each plant
- Add 1 rep per week per plant — cap at ~10 reps per plant per session
- Study slow-motion footage of your own jumps
- Progress: one-hand reach → alley-oop with arms free (arms free = significantly more force)

**Key technical cue**: Great jumpers show **heel/knee lift in the air** on approach jumps — indicates hamstring/posterior chain loading. Straight-legged jumpers are "running at the rim" out of fear rather than being athletic in the air.

## Actionable Advice

- **Start every workout with the sled** (or sled alternatives) — 100 yards per set, multiple rounds
- **Backward sled/treadmill for knee pain**: 4 months of consistent use drives blood flow deep into the joint for healing, not just muscle strengthening
- **Add chains to full-depth squats** to load the entire range of motion — addresses the weakness at the bottom while still challenging the top
- **Train hip flexion directly** with weighted cable or machine hip flexor raises — this is a critical gap for most weight room athletes
- **Test your calf strength**: If you can't complete a single heavy single-leg calf raise (e.g., 44 lb added), that is a direct limiter on your jump
- **Jump every day you're on the court** — start with 1 rep per plant, add 1 rep/week
- **Film your jumps in slow motion** — self-coaching from video is more effective than most coaching
- **Free the arms**: Practice approach jumps without a ball first to learn to use arm swing fully
- **Upper body**: Prioritise weighted dips (chest below elbow) and chin-ups (pecs to bar) for proportional upper body that contributes to arm-swing power

## Things to Avoid

- **Partial-range squats as the primary strength tool** — only trains 2 of 6 zones; misleads athletes into thinking they're jump-training
- **Assuming genetics cap your vertical** — the six-zone framework is the intervention most athletes have never applied
- **Ignoring the weaker plant leg** — imbalances of up to 13 inches in jump height between legs are common and go unnoticed; this also increases injury risk up the chain (knee, spine)
- **Running straight at the rim** — jumping with a rigid, straight leg indicates a fear-based approach rather than athletic loading; kills hang time
- **High jump volume too early** — cap at 10 reps per plant; don't rush the progression

## Key Takeaways

- **Most athletes train only 2 of 6 jumping zones** — the six-zone framework (ankle, knee, hip × flex/extend) is the core reason people plateau and why targeted training produces dramatic results
- **Backward loading (sled, step-up, treadmill) is foundational** — it heals knees and builds strength in the exact position where jumping stress occurs
- **Full-range squatting with chains** bridges the gap between heavy partial squats and the deep-range strength actually needed to jump
- **Jump balance (all four plants)** is the most overlooked skill — even strong athletes are often 10+ inches lower off their weaker leg, and fixing this directly improves both performance and injury resilience
- **Free arms + athletic air mechanics** (heel lift, knee bend) are the technical difference between "going up" and actually jumping — coordination and strength together unlock the ceiling
