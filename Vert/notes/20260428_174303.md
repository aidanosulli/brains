# Vertical Jump Training: 5+ Inches in 30 Days

## Core Principles

- **Measure before you train** — you cannot track progress without a baseline; never start a training program without knowing your starting point
- **Do the thing** — the primary training stimulus should mimic the exact skill being developed; to improve vertical jump, prioritise vertical jumping
- **Progressive isolation** — break the jump down into its components, master each, then integrate them
- **Bilateral training** — always work both sides; asymmetry between left and right approach is common and both sides benefit from training

---

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### Measurement Options (choose one)
1. **Tape method** — stand ~1 ft from wall, mark standing reach with tape, jump and mark max reach, measure the gap with a tape measure
2. **Backboard reference** — film yourself touching as high as possible on the backboard; use that visual mark as a reference point
3. **Jump mat** (gym equipment) — e.g. Just Jump Mat for precise measurement

### The 25–30 Minute Workout (3 phases)

1. **Phase 1 — Do the Thing (5–10 min)**
   - Stand directly under the net
   - Jump straight up, reaching as high as possible every rep
   - Rest when fatigued (1.5–2 min breaks); this is *not* endurance work
   - Goal: beat the previous rep's height each jump

2. **Phase 2 — Two-Step Isolation (10 min)**
   - Start in an athletic/squatted posture
   - Fall into a two-step approach (right leg forward → left right jump, then reverse)
   - Focus on the shin drop and the horizontal-to-vertical transfer of energy
   - 5 minutes on each side (right leg forward, then left leg forward)

3. **Phase 3 — Full Approach Jumps (10 min)**
   - Tie together the work from Phase 2 into a full run-up
   - 5 minutes leading with left-right approach, 5 minutes with right-left approach
   - Applies to both one-foot and two-foot jumpers

### Progress Tracking Cycle
- Train for **2 weeks** → **remeasure** → adjust accordingly
- Repeat: **Measure → Work → Remeasure**

---

## Actionable Advice

- Do this workout **3–6 days per week** (Pat did 6 days/week and gained 5.3 inches in 30 days)
- Rest between jumps whenever form or height degrades — quality over quantity
- Always work **both sides** of the two-step and full approach, even if you're a natural one-foot jumper
- Leave your standing-reach tape mark on the wall so remeasurement is frictionless
- Add supplementary strength training alongside this protocol for compounded results

---

## Things to Avoid

- Starting training without establishing a **baseline measurement**
- Treating jump training as **endurance/conditioning work** — do not grind through fatigue; rest and reset
- Only training your **dominant approach side** — bilateral work is essential
- Skipping the **two-step isolation phase** — this is where most athletes leave gains on the table (horizontal-to-vertical transition is the most critical mechanical moment)
- Neglecting to **remeasure** — without re-testing you cannot know what's working

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Measurement is non-negotiable** — a starting baseline and regular re-testing are what make any training programme accountable and adjustable
- **Specificity works** — the most effective way to improve a vertical jump is to repeatedly perform vertical jumps at maximum effort, not just lift weights or do generic athletic training
- **Isolating the last two steps** is a high-leverage, underused technique that directly trains the horizontal-to-vertical energy transfer — the most mechanically critical moment in any jump
- **25–30 minutes, 3–6x/week** is sufficient stimulus; volume and simplicity beat complexity
- Results are achievable for shorter athletes — a 5'8" athlete added 5.3 inches in 30 days using only this framework
