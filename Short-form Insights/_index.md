# Sailing Upwind: Course Monitoring Fundamentals

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

**Three-Point Upwind Monitoring System** — check these continuously while on an upwind course:

1. **Angle of Heel**
   - Boat should maintain a consistent heel when sailing upwind
   - If heel suddenly drops off → likely in irons or sail trim is incorrect

2. **Boat Speed**
   - Dropping speed = pointing too high / entering the no-sail zone
   - Correction: fall off (bear away) to rebuild speed

3. **Jib Telltales** (leading edge of jib)
   - Jib breaks before the mainsail — use it as your primary indicator
   - **Outside telltale breaks** → head up slightly
   - **Inside telltale breaks** → fall off slightly
   - Note: mainsail telltales sit on the **aft (trailing) edge**

## Actionable Advice

- Monitor all three indicators (heel, speed, telltales) **continuously** on an upwind course — not just occasionally
- Assign crew to trim both jib and mainsail, but **prioritise jib telltales** as your real-time pointing reference
- Use telltale feedback as a micro-adjustment tool — small, reactive heading changes keep you in the optimal sailing lane

## Key Takeaways

- Consistent heel angle is a quick signal of whether the boat is balanced and in a good sailing position upwind
- Boat speed is an early warning system for over-pointing — speed loss should trigger an immediate bear-away
- The jib telltale is the most sensitive and actionable feedback tool for fine-tuning upwind heading
- All three signals work together; monitoring them in combination keeps the boat fast and on course

# Sailing Basics – Harbor to Ocean Vlog

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

- **Orientation on the boat:**
  - Left side = **Port**
  - Right side = **Starboard**
- **Tacking:** the maneuver where the sail switches sides
  - Crew should sit on the **opposite side from the sail** to balance the boat
  - When tacking, move to the correct side as the sail crosses over
- **Hiking:** a technique used when wind picks up
  - Pull on the main sail and lean body out over the water to counterbalance the heel of the boat

## Actionable Advice

- Learn and use correct nautical terminology before heading out (port/starboard, tacking) — especially important once conditions get hectic in open water
- When sailing in wind, practice hiking to keep the boat balanced and responsive
- Sit on the windward side (opposite the sail) as a default crew position

## Key Takeaways

- **Port = left, starboard = right** — foundational orientation every sailor must know
- **Tacking** is the sail switching sides; crew must move with it to maintain balance
- **Hiking** — leaning out over the water while holding the main — is a core skill and one of the most exhilarating parts of sailing
- Always learn basic lingo and safety positions while still in the calm of the harbour before heading into open water

# Pacific Crossing: Tahiti to Washington via Hawaii

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

**Route & Passage Structure**
- **Leg 1 – Morea (French Polynesia) → Hawaii**
  - Upwind sailing in 25 knots with solid swell — a wet, demanding start
  - Passed through the doldrums → crossed the equator → entered Northern Hemisphere
  - Duration: **16 days at sea**
  - Landfall: Molokai cliffs visible first, then Honolulu
- **Leg 2 – Hawaii → Pacific Northwest (Washington)**
  - Conditions progressively colder, greyer, foggier
  - Closest land at points: Alaska
  - Notable wildlife: frequent whale sightings
  - Duration: **~17 days** (33 days total minus 16)
- **Total passage time: 33 days at sea** (plus a pit stop in Hawaii)

**Preparation Phase**
- Several weeks of boat prep in Morea before departure

## Key Takeaways

- The Tahiti-to-US Pacific crossing is unusually demanding because it requires **sailing upwind for thousands of miles** before picking up westerly winds toward the coast
- Passage planning involves distinct phases: upwind slog → doldrums → trade winds → cold North Pacific
- The **80-foot classic yacht *Latifa*** demonstrated that delivery passages on large classic vessels require a seasoned crew and extended prep time
- Conditions shift dramatically across the passage — from tropical South Pacific to cold, fog-bound North Pacific near Alaskan latitudes
- Ocean crossings require mental and physical adjustment; finding "sea legs" typically takes roughly the **first week**
