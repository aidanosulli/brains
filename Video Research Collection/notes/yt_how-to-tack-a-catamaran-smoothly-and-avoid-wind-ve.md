# How to Tack a Catamaran Smoothly and Avoid Wind Veining

> **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3NMS96EsfOQ
> **Date:** 2026-05-29

CONFIDENCE: medium

## Core Principles

- Catamarans require **speed maintenance** through a tack — unlike monohulls, the turn is a gradual arc, not a sharp pivot
- The large mainsail on a catamaran creates **wind veining** (the sail pulls the bow back into the wind), which must be actively counteracted
- Crew coordination and communication are essential for a clean tack

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

**Pre-Tack Setup**
1. Bring the boat to ~**50° to the wind** (close haul) before initiating — some larger production catamarans may need **60°**
2. Center the boom; bring the traveler so the boom runs down the centreline
3. Assign crew roles:
   - One person on the **loaded jib sheet** (ready to release)
   - One person on the **lazy jib sheet** (pre-loaded on the winch, ready to trim)
   - Helmsman identifies the **destination heading** — ~100° away from current heading (50° on the new tack)

**Executing the Tack**
1. If speed is low, **fall off 5–10°** to power up before initiating
2. Helmsman calls *"Ready to tack?"* → crew responds *"Ready"* → Helmsman calls *"Tacking"*
3. Begin a **gradual, arcing turn** (not a sharp snap)
4. **Hold the jib** (backwind it) until the bow passes through **30° to the wind** — this counters wind veining
5. Release the loaded jib sheet once past 30°; new sheet crew begins trimming
6. Helmsman continues steering down to **~60° to the wind** before straightening — do not level off at 30–40°
7. Once settled at 60°, trim the jib in fully, then slowly head back up to close haul (~50°)

## Actionable Advice

- Pre-load the lazy jib sheet on the winch **before** the tack begins
- If the boat feels underpowered approaching the tack, **fall off 5–10° first** to build speed
- Helmsman should pick a **visual reference point** on the new tack heading (100° from current course) before initiating
- Keep steering **past 30°, all the way to 60°** after the bow crosses the wind — this is the most commonly missed step

## Things to Avoid

- **Heading up too early** (to ~40°) while preparing to tack — this depower the sails and kills speed, causing the tack to stall mid-turn
- **Releasing the jib too early** — letting it go before 30° allows wind veining to pull the bow back into irons
- **Straightening out at 30–40°** after the tack — the boat will wind-vein back into irons; must bear away to 60° first
- Treating a catamaran tack like a monohull tack — the mechanics and timing are fundamentally different

## Key Takeaways

- Tacking a catamaran is an **arcing, speed-dependent manoeuvre** — momentum must be preserved throughout
- **Backwinding the jib past 30°** is the critical technique that prevents wind veining from stalling the tack
- The helmsman must **oversteer to ~60°** on the new tack before straightening up — stopping too early is the most common failure point
- Falling off **5–10° before initiating** is a reliable fix when the boat lacks sufficient speed to complete the tack
- Clean tacks come from **crew role clarity and practised timing** — assign positions and rehearse the sequence
