# How to Tack a Catamaran Smoothly — Crew Roles and Technique

> **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3NMS96EsfOQ
> **Date:** 2026-05-29

CONFIDENCE: medium

## Core Principles

- Catamarans require **more speed and a gentler, arcing turn** than monohulls — tacking is not a sharp manoeuvre
- **Wind veining** is the primary hazard: the large mainsail can pull the boat back into irons mid-tack
- Backwinding the jib and steering past the target angle are the two key counters to wind veining

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

**Pre-Tack Setup**
- Head up to **50° off the wind** (60° for larger production catamarans)
- Centre the boom; bring the traveller in to put the boom on the centreline
- Assign crew roles: one person on the loaded jib sheet, one on the lazy jib sheet (or one person handles both if space is limited)
- Helmsman identifies the exit bearing — **~100° from the current heading** (50° on the new tack)
- Load the lazy sheet onto the winch; have the loaded sheet ready to release

**Executing the Tack**
1. If speed is low, **fall off 5–10°** to build momentum before initiating
2. Helmsman calls *"Ready to tack?"* → crew responds *"Ready"* → Helmsman calls *"Tacking"* (or *"Helms alee"*)
3. Begin a **smooth, arcing turn** — not a sudden pivot
4. **Hold the jib backwinded** until the boat passes through **30° to the wind**
5. Release the old jib sheet at 30°; new sheet crew begins trimming
6. Helmsman **continues steering to ~60°** before straightening — do not level off at 30–40°
7. Once locked at 60°, trim the jib in fully, then gradually head back up to a close haul (~50°)

## Actionable Advice

- Fall off 5–10° before tacking if boat speed feels insufficient — better to enter fast than stall mid-tack
- Keep the jib sheeted on the old side until **at least 30°** past head-to-wind to combat wind veining
- Helmsman must **oversteer to 60°** on the new tack before easing back up — this is the most commonly missed step
- Practise crew communication using a clear call-and-response sequence before the manoeuvre begins

## Things to Avoid

- **Slowly heading up to 40°** in preparation — this depowers the sails and causes the boat to stall mid-tack
- **Releasing the jib too early** — allows wind veining to take hold before the boat has committed to the new tack
- **Straightening course at 30–40°** — wind veining will pull the boat back into irons; must bear away to 60° first
- Treating a catamaran tack like a monohull tack — the dynamics are fundamentally different

## Key Takeaways

- Catamarans tack on a **wide, speed-preserving arc**, not a sharp pivot; maintain speed throughout
- The large mainsail causes **wind veining** — backwinding the jib and oversteering to 60° are the essential counters
- Crew coordination and clear role assignment (loaded sheet, lazy sheet, helm) are critical to a clean tack
- The sequence — build speed → arc through → hold jib → oversteer → trim in → head up — must be executed in order
- Smooth, practised tacks feel effortless; the technique is learnable with deliberate crew drills
