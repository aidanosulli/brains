# Three-Phase Blueprint for Increasing Vertical Jump and Dunking

> **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JVQW9H7gc0o
> **Date:** 2026-05-24

## Core Principles

- **Genetics sets your ceiling, but improvement is always within your control** — regardless of starting vertical or age
- **You only fail if you stop** — plateaus are normal and not indicators of failure; PRs often come years after apparent stagnation
- **Specificity of adaptation** — the body adapts only as much as its environment demands; training must be specific and varied to keep driving progress
- **Strength is non-negotiable** — no athlete has ever been "too strong" while still jumping regularly at least once per week
- **Shorter athletes must be relatively stronger** — athletes under 5'9" need higher strength-to-bodyweight ratios than taller athletes to achieve comparable verticals

---

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### Three-Phase Vertical Jump Development

**Phase 1 — Build the Engine (Strength)**
- Goal: produce maximum force
- Two sub-phases:
  - *Hypertrophy*: grow the muscle
  - *Intensity*: train the nervous system to use that muscle efficiently
- Target muscles: hips, hamstrings, calves, quads
- Lift 2–3x per week
- Avoid **block periodization** (all strength → all jumping with nothing in between) — it causes plateaus

**Phase 2 — Build the Springs (Tendons & Nervous System)**
- Mechanism: overload the **stretch shortening cycle** (fast eccentric → fast concentric) to store and release elastic energy in tendons
- Most specific plyometric: max-effort approach jumps (e.g., dunking attempts), even on a lowered rim
- Jump forces can exceed **10x bodyweight** off one foot — reason why strength base must exist first to avoid injury
- Progression:
  - Start at **1 jump session/week**
  - Increase volume by **~10% per week**
  - Build toward **2 sessions/week** (elite standard); very young athletes may handle 3–4
  - Build over months, not weeks

**Phase 3 — Surf the Force-Velocity Curve (Power / Rate of Force Development)**
- The gap between max strength and jumping: a squat takes seconds; a jump ground contact is **~0.2 seconds**
- Being strong but slow = leaving vertical on the table
- Key exercise: **Power Clean**
  - Ground contact time of the double knee bend ≈ jump ground contact time
  - First pull → double knee bend mirrors penultimate step → plant motor pattern
  - Catching a heavy clean = eccentric overload
  - Study of 1,000+ athletes: power clean had the **highest correlation to vertical** of any weight room exercise
- Also reference: **vertical jump deficit training** — identify whether your weakness is strength, rate of force development, or technique, then address that first

### Force-Velocity Curve
- Heavier load = lower velocity; lighter load = higher velocity
- Categorise exercises along this spectrum to bridge max strength → explosive jumping

---

## Actionable Advice

- **Lower your rim** and practice max-effort dunking attempts daily/regularly — the inability to dunk forces maximum intent on every jump
- **Identify your deficit first**: weakness in max strength, rate of force development, or technique — train accordingly
- **Learn and implement power cleans** — teachable to beginners in ~20 minutes; do not dismiss as too complex
- **Build jump volume progressively**: start at 1 session/week, add ~10% weekly
- **Lift 2–3x/week concurrently** with jump training — do not separate them into strict blocks
- Watch oldest-to-newest footage of elite dunkers (e.g., Jordan Kilganon) to internalise that elite athletes started from zero
- Listen to your body — knee pain is a signal to reduce jump frequency and increase strength work, not push through

---

## Things to Avoid

- **Block periodization** — doing all strength work, then switching entirely to jumping with no bridging phase; leads to plateaus
- **Skipping the strength phase** and jumping straight into plyometrics — leads to overuse injuries (e.g., chronic knee pain, IT band tears)
- **Comparing your strength standards to a taller athlete's** if you are shorter — the bar is higher for you relative to bodyweight
- **Dismissing the power clean** as too complicated — complexity is a feature, not a bug; learning difficult motor patterns improves general athleticism
- **Quitting during plateaus** — verticals can stall for 2–3 years and then PR; tendons and nervous system are still adapting even when measurable results stall

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Vertical improvement follows a three-phase structure**: build maximum strength → develop elastic tendon/nervous system capacity → bridge the gap with power (rate of force development), primarily via Olympic lifts
- **Power cleans are the single best weight room predictor of vertical jump**, outperforming all other exercises in correlation studies
- **Shorter athletes need disproportionately higher relative strength** than taller athletes to reach equivalent jump heights
- **Plateaus lasting years are normal** — elite PRs tend to come ~5 years after a squat PR and ~1–2 years after a power clean PR; consistent training is never wasted
- **The only true failure is quitting** — injuries, stalled progress, and long plateaus are part of the process, not endpoints
