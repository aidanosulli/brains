# Vertical Jump Training: Three Core Workouts

## Core Principles

- **Four Laws of Adaptation** govern all effective training:
  - **Progressive Overload** – training must get progressively harder
  - **Variety** – change stimuli to continue causing adaptations
  - **Specificity** – the more exercise resembles jumping, the greater the transfer (fatigue is also specific, so short-term performance may dip before gains arrive)
  - **Individuality** – athletes respond differently to the same stimulus

- **Three physical qualities** determine jump height:
  - **Max Strength** – raw force production capacity
  - **Power** – rate of force development (applying force quickly within the ~0.3s jump window)
  - **Elasticity** – energy stored and returned via the stretch-shortening cycle

- **Strength is foundational**: your legs must essentially squat your own bodyweight explosively; strength training improves rate of force development directly, especially for beginners

- **Shorter athletes require higher relative strength** than taller athletes to achieve the same jump performance, due to shorter lever arms generating less torque per unit of muscle tension

- **Plyometrics train the nervous system**, not the tendons. Max strength work stiffens tendons; plyometrics teach muscles to contract quickly enough to load those tendons optimally

- **Long Conjugate Sequence System**: all three qualities are trained in every session, but the emphasis shifts — each month's focus sets up better gains in the next phase

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### Force-Velocity Curve Periodisation
- Month 1 → **Max Strength** (high force, low velocity)
- Progressively surf toward → **Power** → **Elasticity** (lower force, higher velocity)
- Each phase potentiates the next; do not jump to elasticity work without 2–3 months of strength and power base

### Five Factors of Training Intensity
1. Load (how heavy)
2. Velocity (how fast)
3. Range of motion
4. Intent (how hard you're trying)
5. Proximity to failure

### Power Sub-Categories
- **Strength-Speed**: heavy load moved quickly (e.g., power clean at ~70% max)
- **Speed-Strength**: light load moved quickly (e.g., squat jump at ~20% max)

### Warm-Up Protocol (same for all three workouts)
1. **Isometrics** (if relevant): 3–5 sets × 30–45 sec for knee and Achilles at ~70% MVC; skip if no tendon history or if cartilage/meniscus/ligament issues
2. **General warm-up**: 5–10 min low-effort bike or jog to light sweat
3. **Jump dynamic flexibility**: 5–10 reps per exercise; avoids the force-output reduction caused by static stretching
4. **Sprint development drills**: improve sprinting technique, low plyometric load, nervous system activation
5. **Sport-specific ramp-up**: gradually increase intensity from low-effort lifts or jumps to session intensity

### Workout 1 — Max Strength
*Goal: nervous system efficiency + muscular strength via heavy, low-rep lifting*

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Load / Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hill sprints | 6 × 15 m | ~1 min rest per 10 m; hill reduces hamstring injury risk |
| Barbell deadlift | 5 × 3 | Last 3 sets at 75% 1RM; flat back is non-negotiable |
| Barbell back squat | 4 × 4 | Last 2 sets at 80% 1RM |
| Nordic hamstring curl | 2 × 5 | Low volume — sprinting already taxes hamstrings |

---

### Workout 2 — Power
*Goal: bridge max strength to elasticity; train rate of force development*

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Load / Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Bounding (plyometric) | 1 × 6–8 | Maintenance volume for elasticity; not high volume |
| Clean pull | 5 × 2 | Last 2 sets at 100% power clean max; moderate pull off floor, aggressive jump at top, keep arms straight |
| Pause back squat | 4 × 4 | ~70% 1RM with 3-sec pause; maximal intent every rep |
| Barbell hip thrust | 2 × 8 | Moderate weight; posterior chain volume |
| Calf raise | 2 × 8 | Moderate weight |

---

### Workout 3 — Elasticity
*Goal: train nervous system to exploit stiffened tendons; speed-strength stimulus*

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Load / Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Flat sprints | 6 × 30 m | Full acceleration into upright sprinting; trains short ground contacts and tendon stiffness |
| Skips for distance | 3 × 60 m | Max intent; trains rapid eccentric-to-concentric energy redirection |
| Barbell squat jump | 5 × 4 | 30% of deep squat max; speed-strength stimulus |
| RDL | 4 × 6 | Very light; move fast with max intent |

## Actionable Advice

- **Build up to working sets** by increasing load ~20% per set, starting at 30–50% of max
- **Deadlift from blocks** if hamstring flexibility prevents a flat back — stretch hamstrings daily until you can pull from the floor
- **Hill sprints before flat sprints**: introduce sprinting with hills first (longer ground contacts, lower injury risk, more strength/power dependent)
- **Track your squat max**: if deep squat is barely above bodyweight, prioritise max strength before anything else
- **Use intent as a cue on every rep** — maximal effort recruits Type II fibres, making them larger and faster over time
- **Do not introduce elastic training until 2–3 months of strength and power work** is completed; you'll be fatigued and the adaptation window requires that base
- **Identify your specific deficit** before programming — mismatch between training focus and actual weakness wastes months

## Things to Avoid

- **Static stretching immediately before strength training or jumping** — research shows temporary reduction in power and force output
- **Excessive hamstring/calf volume on top of high sprint volume** — sprinting already trains these muscles; stacking heavy accessory work raises injury risk (per Ralph Omen, Chinese Olympic sprint team methodology)
- **Training max strength with easy, submaximal effort** — sets should leave only 1–2 reps in reserve; anything easier stalls progress
- **Pulling the bar off the floor too fast in clean pulls** — use moderate speed off the floor, then express max intent after the double knee bend / bar passes the kneecap
- **Early arm pull in the power clean** — drill keeping arms straight to ingrain the correct motor pattern
- **Chasing taller athletes' strength numbers** — shorter athletes need proportionally higher relative strength due to lever mechanics
- **Skipping the warm-up or treating it as trivial** — it is intentionally long and hard to function as a training stimulus; deload periods remove this volume to drive supercompensation

## Key Takeaways

- **Max strength is the lowest-hanging fruit** for most athletes — if your squat is near bodyweight, no amount of plyometrics will compensate; get stronger first
- **The three qualities (strength → power → elasticity) must be developed in sequence**, each phase potentiating the next via the force-velocity curve
- **Every session should touch all three qualities** (conjugate approach), but the dominant emphasis rotates by training phase
- **Intent is the single most controllable variable** — maximal intent on every rep is what recruits and develops Type II muscle fibres regardless of the workout type
- **Deficit identification is critical**: training the wrong quality wastes time; diagnose whether your limiter is strength, power, or elasticity before designing a programme

# Nate Kenny: The Making of an Elite Jumper

## Core Principles

- **Obsession over talent** — Elite jumpers are not necessarily born gifted; they are defined by an irrational love for the craft practiced without external validation
- **Long-term compounding** — Years of foundational work (jump reps, movement quality, strength) may show no visible progress but create the biological and neurological substrate for explosive later gains
- **Intrinsic motivation is non-negotiable** — Progress sustained through love of the craft outlasts progress driven by external rewards (views, claps, recognition)
- **Consistency through adversity** — Injuries, plateaus, bullying, and lack of traction are normal checkpoints; quitting at these points is the primary reason most athletes never reach their potential
- **Late bloomers are real** — Not maturing early is not a disqualifier; the gap closes when physical maturity meets years of accumulated skill and strength

---

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### Nate's Development Timeline
- **Age 3–2017:** Dunking on mini hoops; jumping every day; no external motivation; builds foundational technique and tendon stiffness
- **2017–2019:** Obsessed but not progressing visibly; consuming jump content (including bad/gimmicky advice); still accumulating thousands of quality jump reps
- **2020:** First injury (MCL sprain) → 1 month off → returns with new account (Hoopin Nate) → viral traction on ESPN, House of Highlights
- **2021:** Blows up online; develops knee tendinopathy (common at 13–14 in high-jumping males); reaches out for coaching; begins structured training **November 14th**
  - Baseline: ~7ft–7'6" dunk height, 87" touch, 27" vertical
- **2022:** Begins lifting; bodyweight fundamentals first; consistently dunks 8ft, first dunk at 8'6"; starts high school (~5'4"); faces online bullying; takes brief reset then returns
- **2023:** Technique improves; adds power cleans, back squats, front squats, RDLs; Google commercial (aired during NBA Playoffs); Gatorade campaign; still not very strong but building base
- **2024:** Strength becomes a real focus; first meeting with coach at dunk camp; 35" vertical tested; grabs and hangs on 10ft rim; first dunks at 9'6" and 9'9"; squats 225 lbs; power cleans 155 lbs; realistic 10ft attempts
- **2025:** 
  - March 17 — first official 10ft dunk
  - May 24 — second 10ft dunk (gap is normal post-first-dunk)
  - Grows to 5'8"; first legitimate punch dunk; 38" vertical
  - Power clean: 155 → 185 → 205 → 210 → **220 lbs**
  - Back squat: 225 → 275 → **285 lbs** (deep)
  - Vertical: **41 inches**
  - Executing: one-handers, two-handers, 360 reverse pump

### Coaching Philosophy (John Evans' Model)
1. **Assess background first** — understand jump history, maturity stage, and movement foundation before programming
2. **Slow cook the progression** — especially for athletes with high jump volume but underdeveloped strength; don't rush loading
3. **Bodyweight before barbell** — establish movement quality (squat, clean patterns) before adding load
4. **Key lifts for jumping:** back squat, front squat, power clean, RDL
5. **Three pillars required simultaneously:** good movement quality + high jump volume + increasing strength

---

## Actionable Advice

- **Jump every day at a young age** — accumulating thousands of reps builds technique, stiffens tendons, and programs motor pathways even when no visible progress occurs
- **Don't hodgepodge programs** — follow one structured, credible plan rather than mixing gimmicky content from multiple sources
- **Keep posting/tracking even with zero traction** — documenting progress builds self-awareness and technique awareness independent of external feedback
- **After a first dunk, expect a long gap before the second** — trust the process; adrenaline spikes during firsts are hard to replicate; continued training is the only solution
- **Prioritise the power clean and deep squat** — these are specifically called out as integral to athletic development for jumping
- **When injured, take the prescribed time off then return** — a month off is recoverable; quitting is not

---

## Things to Avoid

- **Consuming gimmicky or salesy jump training content** — widespread bad information online will waste training time without producing results
- **Mixing pieces from multiple programs** without a coherent structure ("hodgepodging")
- **Quitting at the first injury** — this is the most common point where developing athletes drop off permanently
- **Seeking external validation as primary motivation** — views, followers, and social traction are unreliable motivators; they disappear during plateaus
- **Rushing strength loading** — athletes with high jump volume but low strength maturity need a "slow cook" approach; premature loading undermines the foundation
- **Interpreting a long plateau as failure** — years of no visible vertical progress can still be productive if movement quality and volume are accumulating

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Talent is not the primary variable** — Nate had no elite physical gifts at the start; consistent obsession over ~10 years produced a 41" vertical at 5'8"
- **Foundational years compound invisibly** — jump reps and movement work done from ages 3–15 paid off explosively once strength maturity arrived; the investment was real even when results weren't visible
- **Three pillars must coexist:** high jump volume, quality movement patterns, and progressive strength training — neglecting any one stalls development
- **Resilience at key failure points is the true differentiator** — injury, bullying, plateaus, and lack of attention are where most athletes quit; Nate's story is defined by not quitting at each of these
- **Late bloomers can reach elite levels** — physical maturity lag is not permanent; when it arrives, it meets years of accumulated skill and the result is rapid, compounding progress
