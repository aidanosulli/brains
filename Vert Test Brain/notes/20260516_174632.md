# Vertical Jump Training: Three Core Workouts

## Core Principles

- **Four Laws of Adaptation** govern all effective training:
  - **Progressive Overload** – training must get progressively harder
  - **Variety** – change stimuli to continue causing adaptations
  - **Specificity** – the more exercise resembles jumping, the greater the transfer (fatigue is also specific, so short-term performance may dip before gains arrive)
  - **Individuality** – athletes respond differently to the same stimulus

- **Three physical qualities** determine jump height:
  - **Max Strength** – raw force production capacity
  - **Power** – rate of force development (applying force quickly within the ~0.3s jump window)
  - **Elasticity** – energy stored and returned via the stretch-shortening cycle

- **Strength is foundational**: your legs must essentially squat your own bodyweight explosively; strength training improves rate of force development directly, especially for beginners

- **Shorter athletes require higher relative strength** than taller athletes to achieve the same jump performance, due to shorter lever arms generating less torque per unit of muscle tension

- **Plyometrics train the nervous system**, not the tendons. Max strength work stiffens tendons; plyometrics teach muscles to contract quickly enough to load those tendons optimally

- **Long Conjugate Sequence System**: all three qualities are trained in every session, but the emphasis shifts — each month's focus sets up better gains in the next phase

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### Force-Velocity Curve Periodisation
- Month 1 → **Max Strength** (high force, low velocity)
- Progressively surf toward → **Power** → **Elasticity** (lower force, higher velocity)
- Each phase potentiates the next; do not jump to elasticity work without 2–3 months of strength and power base

### Five Factors of Training Intensity
1. Load (how heavy)
2. Velocity (how fast)
3. Range of motion
4. Intent (how hard you're trying)
5. Proximity to failure

### Power Sub-Categories
- **Strength-Speed**: heavy load moved quickly (e.g., power clean at ~70% max)
- **Speed-Strength**: light load moved quickly (e.g., squat jump at ~20% max)

### Warm-Up Protocol (same for all three workouts)
1. **Isometrics** (if relevant): 3–5 sets × 30–45 sec for knee and Achilles at ~70% MVC; skip if no tendon history or if cartilage/meniscus/ligament issues
2. **General warm-up**: 5–10 min low-effort bike or jog to light sweat
3. **Jump dynamic flexibility**: 5–10 reps per exercise; avoids the force-output reduction caused by static stretching
4. **Sprint development drills**: improve sprinting technique, low plyometric load, nervous system activation
5. **Sport-specific ramp-up**: gradually increase intensity from low-effort lifts or jumps to session intensity

### Workout 2 — Power
*Goal: bridge max strength to elasticity; train rate of force development*

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Load / Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Bounding (plyometric) | 1 × 6–8 | Maintenance volume for elasticity; not high volume |

| Pause back squat | 4 × 4 | ~70% 1RM with 3-sec pause; maximal intent every rep |
| Barbell hip thrust | 2 × 8 | Moderate weight; posterior chain volume |
| Calf raise | 2 × 8 | Moderate weight |

### Workout 3 — Elasticity
*Goal: train nervous system to exploit stiffened tendons; speed-strength stimulus*

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Load / Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Flat sprints | 6 × 30 m | Full acceleration into upright sprinting; trains short ground contacts and tendon stiffness |
| Skips for distance | 3 × 60 m | Max intent; trains rapid eccentric-to-concentric energy redirection |
| Barbell squat jump | 5 × 4 | 30% of deep squat max; speed-strength stimulus |
| RDL | 4 × 6 | Very light; move fast with max intent |

## Actionable Advice

- **Build up to working sets** by increasing load ~20% per set, starting at 30–50% of max
- **Deadlift from blocks** if hamstring flexibility prevents a flat back — stretch hamstrings daily until you can pull from the floor
- **Hill sprints before flat sprints**: introduce sprinting with hills first (longer ground contacts, lower injury risk, more strength/power dependent)
- **Track your squat max**: if deep squat is barely above bodyweight, prioritise max strength before anything else
- **Use intent as a cue on every rep** — maximal effort recruits Type II fibres, making them larger and faster over time
- **Do not introduce elastic training until 2–3 months of strength and power work** is completed; you'll be fatigued and the adaptation window requires that base
- **Identify your specific deficit** before programming — mismatch between training focus and actual weakness wastes months

## Things to Avoid

- **Static stretching immediately before strength training or jumping** — research shows temporary reduction in power and force output
- **Excessive hamstring/calf volume on top of high sprint volume** — sprinting already trains these muscles; stacking heavy accessory work raises injury risk (per Ralph Omen, Chinese Olympic sprint team methodology)
- **Training max strength with easy, submaximal effort** — sets should leave only 1–2 reps in reserve; anything easier stalls progress
- **Pulling the bar off the floor too fast in clean pulls** — use moderate speed off the floor, then express max intent after the double knee bend / bar passes the kneecap
- **Early arm pull in the power clean** — drill keeping arms straight to ingrain the correct motor pattern
- **Chasing taller athletes' strength numbers** — shorter athletes need proportionally higher relative strength due to lever mechanics
- **Skipping the warm-up or treating it as trivial** — it is intentionally long and hard to function as a training stimulus; deload periods remove this volume to drive supercompensation

## Key Takeaways

- **Max strength is the lowest-hanging fruit** for most athletes — if your squat is near bodyweight, no amount of plyometrics will compensate; get stronger first
- **The three qualities (strength → power → elasticity) must be developed in sequence**, each phase potentiating the next via the force-velocity curve
- **Every session should touch all three qualities** (conjugate approach), but the dominant emphasis rotates by training phase
- **Intent is the single most controllable variable** — maximal intent on every rep is what recruits and develops Type II muscle fibres regardless of the workout type
- **Deficit identification is critical**: training the wrong quality wastes time; diagnose whether your limiter is strength, power, or elasticity before designing a programme
