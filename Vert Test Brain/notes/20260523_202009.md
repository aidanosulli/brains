# Science-Based Framework for Maximising Vertical Jump Training

> **Source:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=KA4eeJJiiRA&pp=ugUHEgVlbi1VUw%3D%3D
> **Date:** 2026-05-23

## Core Principles

- **Genetics are not a barrier**: Fiber type is dictated by the nervous system, not fixed biology. Type 1 fibers can be trained to behave more like Type 2, and Type 2 fibers can be hypertrophied significantly
- **Fast twitch fibers require high-intensity stimulation**: Moderate-load, moderate-pace training primarily trains slow-twitch (Type 1) fibers and does little for explosive power
- **Force development exists on a continuum**: Rather than separate "buckets" (strength, power, elasticity), these are all expressions of force — varying by how fast it's applied and how much is generated
- **Ground contact time is the governing constraint**: Elite 2-foot jumpers are on the ground 0.25–0.29s; 1-foot jumpers 0.17–0.20s. Training must account for this time constraint, not just raw force output
- **Tendons are as important as muscles**: Stiffer, more resilient tendons store and release elastic energy, producing force beyond muscular output alone
- **Plyometrics self-intensify**: As ability improves, plyometric loads increase naturally — creating a compounding adaptation loop

## Key Frameworks & Progressions

### 5-Month Training Block (Linear Periodisation with Long Conjugate Sequences)

| Month | Focus | Method |
|-------|-------|--------|
| 1 | Hypertrophy + tendon resilience | High volume, moderate weight (sets of 10+) |
| 2 | Max strength | 1–5 reps, 80%+ of 1RM, high intent/speed per rep |
| 3 | Rate of Force Development (RFD) | Olympic lifts, loaded jumps |
| 4 | Elastic/plyometric emphasis | Eccentric-focused plyos, jump sessions |
| 5 | Taper | General, low-volume training; allow full recovery and expression |

### Long Conjugate Sequencing
- All qualities trained simultaneously — just at **varying volumes** depending on the current phase emphasis
- In a max strength phase: still jump, but shorter sessions; still do volume work, but perhaps 2–3 sets of one quad exercise on a Saturday
- In a plyometric phase: still touch max strength (e.g., 2×5 hip thrust or half squat), but bulk of work is power and plyos
- Prevents complete detraining of any quality while directing primary adaptation

### Athlete-Specific Training Priority
- **Very strong but low RFD** → prioritise power and elasticity work first
- **Elastic/good plyometric athlete but weak** → max strength is the lowest-hanging fruit
- **Good numbers across the board but poor technique** → technique work first before adding more load

### Type 2 Fiber Development (Two Mechanisms)
1. **Hypertrophy of existing Type 2 fibers** — increase their cross-sectional volume even if fiber count is lower
2. **Train Type 1 fibers to express Type 2 characteristics** — via high-intent, high-load, fast-contraction training

## Actionable Advice

- **For Type 2 hypertrophy**: 1–5 reps at 80%+ 1RM, push every rep maximally fast; accumulate 10–20 sets/week per relevant muscle group
- **For RFD training**, prioritise:
  - Power snatch, power clean and derivatives (clean pull, high clean pull, panda pull, hang clean, block variations)
  - Loaded jumps: barbell squat jumps (light to ~80% half-squat max)
  - Plyometrics: both concentric-focused and true plyos with eccentric overload (e.g., repeated single-leg jumps, depth jumps)
- **Don't skip Month 1**: Jumping straight to heavy 1–5 rep work without a hypertrophy/tendon base leads to injury
- **Use dunking as a plyometric**: Builds intent, self-intensifies weekly as you improve, and is essentially a new stimulus each session
- **Identify your deficit first**: Assess whether your limiter is strength, RFD, elasticity, or technique — then prioritise accordingly
- **Stiffen tendons**: Heavy strength training builds tendon stiffness; accentuated eccentrics increase it further

---

## Things to Avoid

- **Worrying about fiber type genetics** — in real-world training, fiber type shifts seen in studies only occur in extreme disuse (paralysis, spinal injury); not relevant to healthy athletes
- **Skipping the hypertrophy/tendon phase** and going straight to heavy lifting — high injury risk ("Snap City")
- **Training only max strength when RFD is the limiter** — returns diminish; wrong tool for the deficit
- **Doing moderate-load, moderate-pace sets of 10 and calling it explosive training** — this trains Type 1 fibers, not Type 2
- **Ignoring jump technique** — even excellent physical numbers yield poor results without efficient movement patterns

---

## Key Takeaways

- Fiber type is neurologically controlled and functionally trainable — genetics set a baseline, not a ceiling; a 24-inch vert can become a 50.5-inch vert
- Fast-twitch development requires high intent + heavy load (80%+ 1RM, 1–5 reps, 10–20 sets/week) — nothing moderate will achieve this
- Jumping ability is constrained by ground contact time (0.17–0.29s), so training must develop force *within that window*, not just absolute force
- Tendons are a performance multiplier — stiff, elastic tendons return more energy per stretch cycle than muscles alone can produce
- Training works best when the specific deficit (strength, RFD, elasticity, or technique) is identified first and targeted directly, not when everyone follows the same programme
